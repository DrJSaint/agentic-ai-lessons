LV is the clear leader. With **2,261 reviews**, it has more than twice as many as the other three combined — this is the single most powerful discoverability signal in the dataset. The trade-off: a 4.1 rating is the lowest of the four, which in a human-facing context might raise eyebrows, but for pure discoverability it is more than adequate.

#### 🥈 Chanel — **Good Core Profile, Fragmented Listing Risk**
The flagship registers well at 897 reviews and 4.2 stars, with a website and hours fully structured. However, the **fragmented listing problem** is a real discoverability liability: a second Chanel property (Fine Jewellery, 25 reviews) sits nearby and could capture search intent meant for the flagship. An AI assistant querying "Chanel Bond Street" might surface either listing — or both, ambiguously. The missing price tier is a minor gap shared with peers. Priority recommendation: consolidate or clearly differentiate the two listings so AI systems and mapping tools route users correctly.

#### 🥉 Dior — **Solid Mid-Tier Discoverable**
Dior lands comfortably in third. 530 reviews and a 4.5 rating give it the best *quality-weighted* signal after LV, and its structured data (website, hours) is clean and complete. No fragmentation issues. The missing price tier is a gap, but a small one given Dior's category is self-evident to most platforms. There is nothing broken here — the gap to LV is simply one of volume.

#### ⚠️ Brunello Cucinelli — **Underdiscoverable for its Market Position**
Brunello Cucinelli is the outlier. With only **40 reviews**, it is dramatically underrepresented in structured public data relative to its actual market stature. An AI agent sorting by review count or engagement would rank it dead last — and might even treat it as a minor or niche retailer rather than a global luxury flagship. Its 4.4 rating is strong, its website and hours are correctly listed, but those signals are almost invisible at this volume. This is a structural discoverability vulnerability. The likely cause is cultural: BC's ultra-high-net-worth clientele is the least likely demographic to leave Google reviews. But the solution is not to chase volume — it's to ensure every other structured attribute (price tier, Google Business categories, editorial links) is maximally filled in to compensate.
